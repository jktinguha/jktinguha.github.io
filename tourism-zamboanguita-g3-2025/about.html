About Us

Zamboanguita, officially known as the Municipality of Zamboanguita (Cebuano: Lungsod sa Zamboanguita; Tagalog: Bayan ng Zamboanguita), is a fourth-class municipality in the province of Negros Oriental, Philippines. According to the 2020 census, it is home to 29,569 people. While modest in size compared to larger municipalities, it possesses a unique charm, historical significance, and a distinct sense of identity that has developed since its establishment in 1866. Unlike larger cities with numerous barangays (local government units), Zamboanguita has only ten, each contributing to its tight-knit community atmosphere.

The town’s history is rich and steeped in legend, with local folklore contributing to its sense of place and identity. The story of how Zamboanguita earned its name is especially captivating and widely recounted among residents and visitors alike. According to local tradition, long before the arrival of the Spaniards on Negros Island, Zamboanguita was already a well-known fishing spot, attracting fishermen from nearby regions, including Mindanao. The fishing grounds off Zamboanguita were abundant, offering a wide variety of marine life, from tugnos (small juvenile gobies) to iho (sharks), drawing people to its shores and fostering a culture of seafaring and fishing.

One particular incident involving a coguita (octopus) left a lasting impression on the fishermen and, eventually, on the town itself. While fishing in the area, a group of Moro fishermen from Mindanao found an octopus tangled in their fishing net. Recognizing it as a different creature due to its lack of gills and tentacles, they decided to separate it from the rest of their catch. As the story goes, the leader of the Moro fishing crew ordered one of his men to go to the shore, find a tree, and “isab-ong ang coguita,” or “hang the octopus.” Thus, the fisherman hung the octopus on a tree, and it became a recurring practice that every time an octopus was caught, it was hung in that same spot. Over time, the place where the octopus was hung became known as “Sab-ongan ug coguita.” Later, as Spanish colonizers established their presence on the island, they adapted the local term to the Spanish language, naming the town “Zamboangaguita.” The name eventually evolved into “Zamboanguita,” the form we recognize today.

An alternative theory for the name comes from a story circulated among locals. This tale suggests that the name “Zamboanguita” has Spanish roots, specifically the diminutive suffix “-ita,” which means “little.” This theory holds that Zamboanguita may have been named after Zamboanga City, a larger city to the south on the island of Mindanao, due to similarities in geography and climate, thus making “Zamboanguita” mean “Little Zamboanga.” Whether the name derives from the iconic octopus incident or reflects a historical link to Zamboanga City, Zamboanguita’s name holds deep cultural meaning and ties the municipality to both its local heritage and broader regional history.

Located about 28 kilometers south of Dumaguete City, the capital of Negros Oriental, Zamboanguita is strategically situated along the island’s southeastern coastline. The location offers a scenic blend of coastal beauty and lush greenery, with access to natural resources that continue to sustain its fishing communities and agricultural activities. Zamboanguita’s ten barangays form a close-knit community, and each barangay has a role in maintaining the cultural and economic life of the town.

The municipality’s natural beauty and resources are among its most valuable assets. Zamboanguita’s proximity to Apo Island, a globally recognized marine sanctuary, has enhanced its reputation as a destination for eco-tourism and environmental conservation. Apo Island, located just a short boat ride from Zamboanguita, is renowned for its vibrant coral reefs and diverse marine ecosystem, attracting scuba divers, snorkelers, and nature enthusiasts from around the world. The preservation of Apo Island has been a priority for both Zamboanguita and the province of Negros Oriental, and local initiatives focus on protecting its delicate coral reefs and the endangered marine species that reside there. This commitment to conservation reflects the broader eco-tourism goals of Zamboanguita, which prioritizes sustainable practices and the involvement of local communities in preserving natural sites.

Zamboanguita’s economy is largely based on agriculture and fishing, with the town’s farmers and fishers contributing significantly to the local economy. Fishing, in particular, remains a central aspect of life in Zamboanguita, with methods and practices passed down through generations, reinforcing a sense of cultural heritage and self-sufficiency. The town’s fishing grounds have provided sustenance and livelihood opportunities for centuries, and today, fishing remains a valued trade. The municipality’s agricultural sector is diverse, producing a range of crops that support both local consumption and trade within the region. The town’s agricultural products, combined with its rich fishing grounds, make Zamboanguita a town capable of self-reliance and resilience in the face of economic changes and challenges.

Additionally, Zamboanguita’s history is intertwined with the Spanish colonial era, which left an indelible mark on the region’s cultural practices, architecture, and language. During the colonial period, many aspects of Spanish culture became embedded in local traditions, from religious celebrations to architectural influences seen in the town’s historical structures. These Spanish legacies are still visible in Zamboanguita today, with the town’s residents continuing to celebrate fiestas and other cultural events rooted in Spanish heritage.

Community life in Zamboanguita is marked by strong ties among residents and a spirit of collaboration that permeates daily life. The town’s barangays are organized to encourage collective decision-making and mutual support, ensuring that local needs and challenges are addressed efficiently. Social gatherings, fiestas, and community events are integral to life in Zamboanguita, where residents come together to celebrate and uphold their cultural traditions. The spirit of hospitality and friendliness is widely recognized as a defining characteristic of Zamboanguita’s people, and visitors often remark on the warm welcome they receive upon arriving in the town.

In recent years, Zamboanguita has made strides in promoting eco-tourism, positioning itself as a destination that values environmental preservation and sustainable tourism practices. This commitment to eco-tourism is seen in efforts to promote responsible tourism on Apo Island and within Zamboanguita itself. Local government programs aim to educate both residents and visitors about the importance of protecting the town’s natural resources and minimizing environmental impact. These initiatives have helped raise awareness about the need for conservation and have encouraged sustainable practices that benefit the local community and the environment.

The municipality also emphasizes the importance of involving local communities in eco-tourism activities. Residents are encouraged to participate in eco-tourism ventures, such as providing homestays, serving as tour guides, and offering local handicrafts for sale. This community-based approach not only provides additional income opportunities for residents but also fosters a sense of pride and responsibility toward preserving Zamboanguita’s natural beauty and cultural heritage.

Zamboanguita’s efforts to develop sustainable tourism align with its broader goals of promoting socio-economic growth and environmental protection. By prioritizing eco-tourism, the municipality aims to attract visitors who are interested in experiencing the area’s natural landscapes, marine biodiversity, and cultural richness without compromising its ecological integrity. This balance between economic development and environmental conservation is central to Zamboanguita’s vision for the future, ensuring that it remains a vibrant and sustainable community for generations to come.

In conclusion, Zamboanguita is a municipality that exemplifies the harmony between cultural heritage, natural beauty, and community-driven development. From its unique name and historical roots to its focus on sustainable tourism and environmental preservation, Zamboanguita offers a compelling example of a town that values both its past and its future. Its residents, deeply connected to the land and sea, embody a spirit of resilience and adaptability that has allowed the town to thrive over the centuries. As Zamboanguita continues to grow and evolve, it remains committed to preserving the cultural and natural heritage that defines its identity, making it a place of both historical significance and enduring charm.
